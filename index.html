A few orbital properties

Today we're going to be talking about three important orbital properties that can tell you almost everything about an orbit!

  1. The two apsides, apoapsis and periapsis

    2. The apsides are the two farthest points of any orbit. The apoapsis is the highest point, leaving the periapsis to be the lowest. In a perfect circular orbit, both apsides will be the same. Pretty simple, right?

  2. Eccentricity

    3. Eccentricity is a property that describes how much an object in orbit deviates from a perfect, circular orbit.

    • A circular orbit has an eccentricity of 0.
    • A non-circular elliptical orbit has an eccentricity between 0 and 1.

    While we're specifically talking about orbits and not trajectories, eccentricity can be applied to more than ellipses.

    • A parabolic trajectory has an eccentricity of 1.
    • A hyperbolic trajectory has an eccentricity greater than 1.

  3. Inclination

    4. Inclination is, simply put, the tilt of an orbit. It's usually given in degrees, and if it's a satellite orbiting a planet, the reference tilt is usually the planet's equator. The inclination can also tell you the rotation of the object in the orbit.

    Inclinations between 0 degrees and 90 degrees are in a prograde orbit (they go around the orbit the same direction the planet rotates), while an inclination between 90 degrees and 180 degrees are in a retrograde orbit (the opposite of a prograde orbit). An inclination of 0 degrees means the satellite is in a prograde orbit going the same way as the planets rotation. An inclination of 90 degrees is a polar orbit (imagine an orbit that is in line with the top and bottom of the planet). An inclination of 180 degrees is an equitorial retrograde orbit.
